# The Board Never Lies

_A fictional first-person essay in the style of The Talker. Used as source material for the Night Dispatch episode._

---

My mother wanted me to be a teacher. I tell people that when they ask how I ended up here, twenty-two years into a job most people don't know exists. Dispatchers. We're the ones in the back, in the room with the board and the radio and the coffee that nobody made fresh. We're the reason your driver knew to take the tunnel instead of the bridge at 2:47 in the morning. Not the app. Us.

I took the job because my cousin Reggie was already doing it and said it was steady. That was it. No calling, no plan. Steady work. I was twenty-six.

The room doesn't look like much. It never did. There's the board — digital now, used to be magnetic strips and a whiteboard, I preferred the whiteboard — and four monitors, and the radio console, and a shelf where people leave things they don't want to take home. Right now there's a half-full bottle of hot sauce, three pens that probably don't work, and a laminated photo of someone's dog that's been there so long nobody remembers whose it was.

The overnight is its own thing. Different city. Not the movie version of different — not glamorous, not dangerous, not silent. Just different mathematics. You're moving maybe eighteen, twenty cars through a city that was built for a million and only has about eighty thousand awake. You'd think that would make it easier. It doesn't. The ones who are awake at 3am are awake for a reason, and the reasons are rarely simple, and simple is the only thing that moves fast.

I know my drivers by their radio habits before I know anything else. How long they wait before confirming a redirect. Whether they say "copy" or just key twice. Whether they tell me things. Some don't — it's professional, it's correct, nothing wrong with it. Position, status, clear. But some talk, and when it's slow and the night is long, I let it go. Marcus will tell me about a fare if it was strange enough. A couple once asked him to drive them around the park three times without stopping and they just sat there looking out opposite windows. He did it. They tipped well. He told me about it a week later, not sure what to make of it. I don't know either. That's the job.

The decline is real and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. We had forty-two drivers on the overnight in 2014. Tonight I've got nineteen. The apps took most of it. I don't have a position on the apps, I just have the numbers. What I know is that my nineteen are the ones who wanted to keep doing it this way, with a dispatcher, with someone on the other end who knows which avenue runs slow on Tuesdays and why. That means something to them. It means something to me.

There's a version of this job that's just logistics. Board goes green, radio crackles, you route, done. I've seen dispatchers do it that way and they last about four years before they hollow out and quit. The job requires you to hold something for the drivers that they can't hold themselves while they're moving. Attention, maybe. Someone knowing where they are. My guys are out there alone at 4am and when they key up and give me a position, what they're really saying is: I'm here. And I come back with a redirect or a hold and what I'm really saying is: I know. I got you.

That's not in any training manual. But it's the whole job.
